brief

New York City has a rich culture of community gardens where people come to maintain their own vegetable and fruit crops. These gardens are dispersed throughout the city and tend to support a large range of other activities and programming such as movie nights, community meals, lectures, teach-ins, workshops, thus serving as a vital neighborhood hub. Many gardens accept food waste from neighboring residents and restaurants to produce compost for the plants within the garden and beyond. However composting can be a time and energy-intensive process, as well as occupy valuable land. A single vertical unit could assist in the processing of food waste while also allowing for a new interesting element in the garden.

For the purpose of the vertical system at the scale of a community garden, it is feasible to construct one of relatively cheap and common materials, permitting an adaptive interface as the individual garden confronts new challenges or responds to new needs. This system is composed of PVC pipe as the structural unit and metal mesh to contain the soil and worms where the composting process occurs.

brief

Between the annual flooding of the Chao Phraya and its tributaries and the impending rising seas, Bangkok is facing its greatest existential threat in its 500 year history. Overreliance on flood control infrastructure has exposed Bangkok to the devastating forces of flood waters. Ultimately the sea poses the greatest threat. Climate change will bring upwards of 2.5 meters in sea level rise along the lower Chao Phraya by the end of this century, inundating the mostly flat plain on which the city is built.

New infrastructures, that accomodate the fluctuations of the river and tides, are necessary to ensure our continued livelihoods without the inevitable destruction of the current land-based solutions. A mega structure, or Super Node, will incorporate industry, agriculture, cultural and recreational facilities for urban dwellers into a contained unit to prevent sprawl and further land degredation allowing for reforestation and wetland restoration within and around the urban center.

brief

Paraisopolis is a favela located in Sao Paulo. It is home to more than 50,000 residents whose homes are sometimes not equipped with runnign water or proper plumbing. The neighborhood has no green space and recreation is limited to the narrow streets that wind up and down the hilly landscape. Most of the houses are constructed of concrete block and masonry as a series of extruded boxes that continue to build upon each other. Community facilities are also scarce, thus a large patch of eroded hillside has been planned to contain a new library and composting that will also provide ample green space, vegetable gardens as well as a soccer field and basketball courts.

There are ten compost towers within the campus in order to handle the entire food waste of Paraisopolis. This will generate ample compost to then provide the opportunity for local and free production of vegetable and fruit for the lower-income residents. The library is integrated into the hillside creating a stepped urban form. The campus is constructed of brick and stone cubes extruded to match the architecture of the neighborhood.
